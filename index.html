Let's get Nuclear!

Welcome to Nuclear Policy and the Climate Crisis interactive game. Here you will have the chance to discover the interdependence of nuclear weapons, nuclear energy and the climate crisis - its common policy issues and dangers. Your goal is to find the perfect policy combination that enables you to regulate nuclear weapons and nuclear energy in order to minimise their adverse impacts on the development of the climate crisis. After the completion of the game, you will be able to consult our research on the explanations for policy inaction and tools for combating it in this area of concern as well. Disclaimer: While this game is based on research done on the topic of the interdependence of nuclear weapons and the climate crisis, it is important to note that the outcomes of this game are speculations of the authors. Furthermore, this game is merely a simplification of a very complex topic, therefore, we offer further educational resources if you would like to learn more about the intersection of nuclear topics and the climate crisis in different stages of the game.

Rules

You will be presented with six different policies which regulate the proliferation of nuclear weapons and nuclear energy in a fictional country of the near future in which mitigation of the climate crisis represents a pressing topic of debate. You will have to choose between three policies focused on nuclear weapons and three policies focused on nuclear energy, while each combination of choices determines a distinct outcome. Your choices will determine the success of your policies in mitigating the climate crisis, so choose wisely! After you make your decisions and learn about their potential implications on the environment, you will be presented with a set of explanations and tools for overcoming inaction on the joint threats of nuclear phenomena and the climate crisis. Having taken them into account, you may choose to play the game again and choose a different policy combination to try to get a more desirable outcome, if you were not satisfied with the consequences of your previous choices.

Short Case Study to Help Inform Your Decisions

If you are unfamiliar with the topic of the intersection between nuclear phenomena and the climate crisis, you might see the potential link between nuclear energy and the environment, but unsure about what the connection between nuclear weapons and the environment might be, or even doubtful that there is any Let's examine the Castle Bravo nuclear test from 1954:

Scope: biggest nuclear detonation executed by the United States, the Castle Bravo test in 1954 during which a thermonuclear bomb of the yield of 15 megatons (meaning 1000x stronger than the bomb detonated over Hiroshima) was detonated on the Bikini Atoll, Marshall Islands

Consequences: The immediate result was radioactive contamination of 7000 square miles of the Pacific as well as complete decimation of oceanic life and global pollution of the atmosphere with soot and radioactive isotopes

Implications: The example of the scale of environmental damage resulting from just one such test demonstrates the fact that, as Rens van Munster says, “modern war (of which nuclear weapons are now a component) is not just a socio-political but also a deeply environmental or ecological problem”

Understanding Inaction: False Assumption of Impossibilit of Action

In terms of climate change, structural obstacles, such as the need for economic growth, are constantly used to explain and justify inaction. Schemes such as a carbon tax or cap-and-trade programs are seen as the best way to fight climate change, since we conceptualised all solutions within our economic framework, however their efficacy is highly debatable as opposed to other solutions which more radically diverge from neoliberalism. Moreover, certain conceptions of morality can impose limitations on our imaginations of effective solutions to the climate crisis and nuclear phenomena. In particular, the notion of “moral purity,” in which individual contributions to mitigating the climate crisis are viewed as an all-or-nothing commitment, can lead to perceiving any progress towards solutions as practically unattainable and create distressing emotional states which further constrain the scope of action. (Sparkman and Attari, 2020 & Wrosch et al., 2003) Similarly, in nuclear policy, the promise of nuclear disarmament has met structural obstacles. For example, President Obama promised disarmament, yet immediately labelled this hope as a ‘distant goal’ in 2009. Thus, we are made to exist in a “nuclear eternity” where no future without nuclear weapons is conceivable (Pelopidas, 2021). The same problem arises with regards to thinking beyond the neoliberal economic system when it comes to solving the climate crisis. In both cases, a transformation of current social norms is required to allow for effective solutions of the joint threat of the climate crisis and nuclear phenomena.

Understanding Inaction: Self-Imposed Limits of Imagination

Günther Anders’ Theses for the Atomic Age (1962) introduces us to the notion of humanity in the atomic age embodying the condition of inverted utopians, since we cannot imagine the reality in which we live and have ourselves created, as opposed to regular utopians who are unable to produce a reality which they succeed in imagining. If we define political imagination as the “ways various agents envision and give sense to their political existence” we start to notice the inhibitions surrounding that vision, namely the tendency to master or conquer the future through the lenses of present convictions. (Hayden in Sylvest and Munster, 2016) This is a tendency which can render unproductive and dangerous consequences. Scholars like Günther Anders or Lewis Mumford emphasise the need to re-open the future in order to surpass the constraints of the presentist normative framework which we now use to picture it, asking us to imagine it rather than to try to conquer it. In other words, instead of thinking within self-imposed boundaries of the impossibility of mitigating the climate crisis or preventing a nuclear disaster, we should dare to imagine a hopeful future and by virtue of overcoming that first cognitive obstacle, start acting on it. To that end, it is useful to consider the phenomenon of imaginative self-censorship which works to constrain the formulation of future scenarios by relying solely on the past and presently knowable, thus significantly impeding the horizons of our imagination when it comes to tackling climate change and nuclear weapons. (Pelopidas, 2016) Furthermore, a staunch belief in technology as the inevitable saving grace of humanity creates a closed conception of the future, in which we cannot divorce progress from perpetual technological development.

Continuation

This is especially problematic, when we adopt an instrumentalist view of nuclear weapons, treating them as objects which do not inherently possess value but are instead imbued with it at the hands of policy-makers, resulting in an assumption of lesser responsibility when it comes to their continued development. Equally, when it comes to proposed solutions of climate change, we tend to develop them within the constraints of faith in technology and market-based capitalism, embodied for example by the green growth approach. As noted by Rens van Munster, “techno-managerial thinking sits at the centre of our ecological problem today,” hence imposes further limits on our imagination. (Munster, 2021) The problem of conceptualising self-extinction is another constraint when it comes to imagining the future of nuclear phenomena and climate change. Jonathan Schell introduces extermination as an invention of the 20th century which does not just denote a crime against the present, but one against the future. (Schell, 2000) The centrality and normalisation of the possibility of nuclear-weapon induced extermination within the current policy framework of not just totalitarian states like North Korea, but also liberal democracies such as the United States makes us confront deterrence as a policy of retaliatory genocide, instead of a peace-inducing tool of international relations. This is because once the scruples that inhibit the extermination of millions of others have been discarded, we find ourselves in a situation in which there are very few left with which to protect ourselves. Schell notes the cognitive gulf that emerged between means and ends when it comes to how we imagine the role of nuclear weapons in our world today, since we the imagined ends of nuclear weapons are “the splendours of liberal civilisation and peace” despite their means being extermination which is so radically evil we struggle to even imagine it. (Schell, 2000)

Implementation of a Holistic Worldview

One of the possible solutions to overcoming inaction on the threats of nuclear weapons and climate change is adopting a holistic worldview. Holism is defined by Sylvest and Munster as a “view of the whole, interconnected world, a concern for future generations and a valuation of the artistic, aesthetic and otherwise non-quantifiable dimensions of human life.” (Sylvest and van Munster, 2016) It is useful here to consider the concept of “intergenerational justice” formulated by Matthew Rendall, since it is only through concern for future generations that we can start conceptualising responsible solutions to the nuclear and climate conundrums. (Rendall, 2007) Additionally, challenging the psychological distance we often experience in imagining the future impacts of the climate crisis, which tends to result in lower concern and motivation to act, can help us overcome inertia. (Trope and Liberman, 2010) Indeed, the recognition of the world as one not only spatially, but also temporally, represents the most important prerequisite for changing it. We can find inspiration for adopting a holistic mindset in the indigenous worldview, such as the Sumak Kawsay, in which nature is ordained with an intrinsic value as opposed to an exploitable resource as is the case in the western worldview.

The Role of Productive Fear and Guilt

Finally, as opposed to viewing fear as a paralysing agent, Günther Anders invites us to perceive it as “helpful in recovering the ability of humans to imagine and feel.” (Anders, 1962) It is this notion of “loving fear” that should inform our action on nuclear weapons and climate change in view of present and future generations of humanity. (Anders, 1962) One must not also abandon hope which forms the crux of the “ethics of possibility” as formulated by Arjun Appadurai. (Appadurai in Sylvest and van Munster, 2016) Such an ethic includes efforts to increase the horizon of hope, expand the imagination and produce greater equity as part of critical global citizenship. In other words, the ethics of possibility labels hope the political counterpart to the work of the imagination. In the expansion of the boundaries of our imagination of the future, we can also turn to the power of fiction to circumvent the boundaries of imagination. One might think of the depictions of post-apocalyptic worlds following a nuclear war depicted in On the Beach (1959). Such works of fiction help us make tangible the radical evil of nuclear weapons by visualising its consequences. Similarly, visualisations of the environmental consequences of nuclear testing using modern technological tools can make the threat more tangible and increase our emotional response, and hence possibly motivation to act on it.
